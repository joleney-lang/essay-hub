Know Your Essay Types

O-Level Paper 1 tests five broad essay types. Understanding which type a question belongs to tells you how to structure your answer.

📖 Narrative
Story-telling with a plot arc. Often fictional.
📝 Personal Recount
A real experience. Reflection and insight matter most.
🎨 Descriptive
Vivid sensory detail of a person, place, or event.
⚖️ Discursive
Explores both sides of an issue with a balanced, nuanced tone.
🗣️ Argumentative
Takes a clear position and defends it with evidence and rebuttals.

Why this site focuses on Discursive & Argumentative essays

Narrative, recount, and descriptive essays draw on personal experience and creativity — best developed through reading and practice. Discursive and argumentative essays require content knowledge, structured argument, and real-world evidence. That is where preparation pays off, and where this hub helps most. Use the Content Bank to build your evidence base. Use the Planner to structure your argument. The writing is always yours.

Arguments, Evidence & News

📱Social Media
✦
Overcomes geographical barriers — instant global connection and civic action
▶

Social media enables real-time connection across countries and empowers ordinary citizens to organise, fundraise, and advocate for change at scale.

Example: Singapore crowdfunding campaigns raised S$2.9 million in 10 days to fund a baby's life-saving treatment via social media. The #MeToo movement went global through Twitter. Greta Thunberg's #FridaysForFuture mobilised millions of youth climate activists worldwide without any institutional support.
✗
Mental health crisis — anxiety, depression, toxic body image
▶

Platforms flood feeds with curated, filtered images setting unrealistic standards — particularly harming teenagers' self-perception and mental wellbeing.

Example: Meta's own leaked internal research (2021) found Instagram worsened body image for 1 in 3 teenage girls. Singapore's IMH National Youth Mental Health Study (2024) found 30.6% of youth aged 15–35 experience severe depression, anxiety, or stress — with social media a key identified driver.
✗
Fake news spreads 6× faster than truth — real-world consequences
▶

Studies show misinformation spreads six times faster than accurate news on social media, with serious consequences for public health and democratic institutions.

Example: Singapore enacted POFMA (2019) to combat online falsehoods. By mid-2024, 163 directions had been issued — over a quarter targeting COVID-19 misinformation. Singapore's 2025 MDDI survey found 84% of residents encountered harmful online content in the past year. In 2025, Singapore also banned deepfakes of election candidates on social media.
✗
Governments worldwide enacting bans — proof that self-regulation has failed
▶

The scale of harm has prompted unprecedented legislative action, signalling that platforms cannot be trusted to protect young users.

Example: Australia (December 2025) became the first country to ban social media for under-16s, covering TikTok, Instagram, YouTube, Snapchat, X, Reddit — with fines up to $50 million AUD. By February 2026, France, UK, Malaysia, Germany, Italy and Spain were considering similar bans. Singapore introduced a new Online Safety Code of Practice for App Distribution Services in early 2025.
💻Devices & Education
✦
Unlimited access to information — self-directed learning at any hour
▶

Students no longer need to wait for a teacher or rely solely on textbooks — search engines and educational apps enable immediate doubt-clearing.

Example: Apps like Snapask provide 24/7 homework help. During Singapore's Home-Based Learning (HBL) in COVID-19, devices were the only link to education — proving they are now essential learning infrastructure, not a luxury.
✗
Addiction and distraction — devices compete directly with studying
▶

The same device used for studying is a gateway to TikTok, games, and social media — all engineered with addictive loops that students struggle to resist.

Example: Singapore's CNA/IPS survey found teenagers spend 8.5 hours daily on devices, with 58.8% using screens to cope with negative emotions. Singapore's Health and Social Development ministers (2024) called for new safeguards citing "deteriorating youth mental health." China legally restricted under-18s to 1 hour of gaming per week. Singapore's MOH issued updated Screen Use guidance in January 2025.
✗
Academic dishonesty — AI and internet make cheating trivially easy
▶

Easy access to AI writing tools has made plagiarism and cheating effortless — depriving students of the cognitive development that genuine effort builds.

Example: ChatGPT can generate a full essay in seconds. Schools worldwide are struggling to distinguish AI-written work from genuine student submissions. Students who outsource thinking to AI bypass the reasoning skills that essay-writing is specifically designed to build.
🌐Technology & Society
✦
Technology improves daily life — convenience, healthcare, connection
▶

From telemedicine to digital banking, technology has made everyday life meaningfully more convenient for most people.

Example: Telemedicine saves unnecessary clinic visits. PayNow enables instant free transfers. Singapore deploys AI fall-detection systems for seniors (CNBC, 2025), helping the elderly age in place safely — a "high tech, high touch" model of care.
✗
Digital divide — elderly and low-income are left behind
▶

Singapore's rapid digital transformation assumes smartphone access and digital literacy — both of which many elderly and lower-income residents lack.

Example: A Singapore Eye Research Institute survey found 55% of seniors over 60 were unlikely to adopt digital health services due to technological difficulties. Older Singaporeans excluded from PayNow and e-government services find technology makes their lives harder, not more fulfilling.

🏃Lifestyle & Health
✗
Teenagers lead increasingly sedentary, screen-dependent lifestyles
▶

The rise of smartphones, streaming, and gaming means teenagers spend significantly more time sitting indoors than any previous generation — with serious physical and mental health consequences.

Example: WHO recommends no more than 2 hours of recreational screen time per day for adolescents — Singapore teenagers average 8.5 hours. Jonathan Haidt's The Anxious Generation (2024) argues the shift from play-based to screen-based childhood is the primary driver of the global youth mental health crisis since 2012.
✦
Today's youth are more aware of mental health and willing to seek help
▶

Today's teenagers are more emotionally literate and open to discussing mental health than any previous generation — partly driven by awareness campaigns and reduced stigma.

Example: Singapore's CHAT (Centre for Youth Mental Health) has supported over 7,600 youths as of March 2024, with an online counselling platform (webCHAT). Singapore's MOH launched "Parenting for Wellness" in September 2024 to help families manage screen time and support youth mental health.
⭐Celebrity & Fame
✗
Celebrity culture promotes unrealistic lifestyles and drives teen anxiety
▶

Social media has made celebrity culture inescapable — and the lifestyles it promotes are financially and psychologically out of reach for most teenagers.

Example: TikTok influencers promote luxury goods to teenagers who cannot afford them, driving impulsive spending and dissatisfaction. "FOMO" (fear of missing out) is now a clinically recognised source of adolescent anxiety, directly linked to social media exposure to celebrity-like peer content.
✦
Some public figures inspire meaningful civic action among youth
▶

Not all celebrity influence is shallow — some public figures use their reach to champion important causes and mobilise young people toward positive change.

Example: Greta Thunberg mobilised millions of youth globally on climate action through social media alone, triggering the global school climate strike movement. Local Singaporean social media figures have raised significant funds for charities and normalised conversations about mental health among youth audiences.
🎓Education & the Real World
✗
Classroom education consistently lags behind real-world skills needs
▶

Many students graduate without the communication, financial literacy, or critical thinking skills that employers consistently identify as lacking.

Example: Singapore's SkillsFuture initiative was created precisely because employers flagged a mismatch between graduate skills and workplace needs. Basic tasks like filing taxes, managing a budget, or writing professional emails are rarely taught in school — which is why the O-Level 2023 question asked about practical skills directly.
✦
Today's youth are globally aware and civically engaged
▶

Compared to previous generations, today's youth are more informed about global issues and more willing to take action — through volunteering, advocacy, and social enterprise.

Example: Singapore's youth volunteer rates are among the highest in Asia. Youth-led social enterprises are growing in number and impact. The global school climate strike movement was entirely organised by teenagers with no adult institutional support — demonstrating genuine agency, not just online slacktivism.

👴Elderly & Ageing
✗
Singapore's ageing population creates major social and economic pressure
▶

By 2030, one in four Singaporeans will be aged 65 or above — straining healthcare systems, eldercare infrastructure, and the working-age population.

Example: Singapore is now a "super-aged" society with over 20% aged 65+ by 2025. Around 6,000 nurses and care staff must be hired annually to meet healthcare targets. Many older Singaporeans lacked sufficient MediSave savings for long-term care — prompting major policy reforms. Young Singaporeans increasingly face "sandwich generation" pressures, caring for both children and elderly parents.
✦
Singapore invests in active ageing and elderly empowerment
▶

Rather than treating the elderly as burdens, Singapore has invested significantly in enabling older Singaporeans to remain productive, active, and connected.

Example: The Senior Employment Credit incentivises hiring of older workers. Active Ageing Centres provide programmes for physical activity, social connection, and mental stimulation. Singapore deploys AI fall-detection and eldercare technology to help seniors age in place safely (CNBC, 2025).
👨‍👩‍👧Family & Community
✦
Family meals build bonds and support children's wellbeing
▶

Research consistently links regular family meals to better academic performance, lower substance abuse rates, and stronger family cohesion across cultures.

Example: A Harvard study found family dinners were the single strongest predictor of children's academic and emotional wellbeing. Singapore's UNESCO-recognised hawker culture reflects how shared meals serve as community-building across race, age, and class — a unique form of social glue in a multicultural society.
✗
Modern work demands increasingly prevent regular family time
▶

Long working hours, both parents working, and children in after-school activities make synchronising family schedules genuinely difficult in modern Singapore.

Example: Singapore consistently ranks among countries with the longest working hours globally. A SUSS study (2024) found young adult caregivers in Singapore face significant role conflict between caregiving obligations and their own career development — reflecting broader family time pressures in a high-pressure urban society.
⚖️Fairness & Happiness
✦
Fairness is fundamental to social trust, cohesion, and stability
▶

Societies where people perceive the system as fair show higher civic participation, lower crime rates, and greater collective wellbeing.

Example: Singapore's meritocracy model — equal opportunity regardless of race or background — has produced remarkable social harmony in a highly diverse society. The Edelman Trust Barometer (2021) found Singapore's trust in government at 76, well above the global average of 53, reflecting the role of perceived fairness in social stability.
✗
Happiness depends on more than fairness — purpose and connection matter equally
▶

Positive psychology shows happiness is determined by a complex mix — fairness is one factor, but meaning, relationships, and autonomy are equally important.

Example: Bhutan measures "Gross National Happiness" rather than GDP, prioritising community wellbeing and spiritual health alongside economic factors. Research shows lottery winners return to their baseline happiness within a year — suggesting happiness is not purely circumstantial or dependent on perceived fairness of outcomes.
